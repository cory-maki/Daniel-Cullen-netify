What Coaching Teaches You About Leadership Off the Clock

📅 May 2025
✎ Daniel J. Cullen
⌚ 5 min read

Most leadership books talk about the office. The ones that changed me most were written on a basketball court and in a boxing gym.

Daniel J. Cullen has spent his career in construction and manufacturing — industries that require hard decisions, clear communication, and teams that trust each other under pressure. But some of his sharpest lessons in leadership have come from completely different arenas: youth basketball with the Waukesha South Blackshirts, and boxing instruction through Rock Steady Boxing in Janesville.

The Gym Doesn't Let You Bluff

In a boardroom, you can hide behind slides and qualifications. In a boxing gym, you can't. When you're coaching a participant with Parkinson's disease through non-contact boxing drills, the feedback is instant and unambiguous. Either your cue worked and they moved better, or it didn't. Either your patience held and they pushed through something hard, or it didn't. The gym cuts through every pretense of leadership and shows you what you're actually made of.

Rock Steady Boxing is a program designed to help individuals with Parkinson's disease improve strength, coordination, and quality of life through boxing-style training. Instructors like Daniel don't just teach technique — they build relationships, hold space for vulnerability, and find ways to challenge people who are fighting something much bigger than a sparring opponent. That's one of the most human things a leader can do. You can follow Daniel's thoughts on athletic discipline at his X account.

"The best coaches don't manufacture dependence. They develop the ability to make good decisions when no one's watching."

Youth Coaching and the Long Game

Youth basketball is a different test. The Waukesha South Blackshirts program gives Daniel a window into how young people respond to authority, accountability, and encouragement — and how quickly they can tell the difference between a coach who's there for them and one who's there for the scoreboard.

The parallel to workplace leadership is direct. Young workers entering manufacturing are not so different from athletes learning the game. They want feedback that's honest without being crushing. They want to understand the "why" behind the play. They respond to coaches who know their names and remember what they told you last week. The details matter — in the gym, and in the shop. These lessons feed directly into how Cullen builds culture at Precision Metal Fab.

Accountability Without Shame

One of the hardest things to get right in any coaching relationship — athletic or professional — is how to hold someone accountable without humiliating them. The instinct is often to correct loudly and publicly. The reality is that shame produces defensiveness, not growth.

Great coaches develop a feel for when to be direct and when to be private. They learn which individuals respond to challenge and which ones need encouragement first. They understand that the same message can land completely differently depending on tone, timing, and the relationship that's been built. Daniel J. Cullen carries all of this into his leadership at Precision Metal Fab in Delafield — and into his faith community work at St. Anthony's on the Lake.

Service as Leadership Practice

There's a specific kind of leadership development that only happens when you're not in charge. When you're volunteering — coaching kids, leading a program for people with Parkinson's, organizing a parish family event — no one owes you their attention or their effort. You earn it, moment by moment, through consistency and care. That's the purest form of leadership there is.

For Daniel, the hours spent coaching are not a break from the work of professional leadership. They are the ongoing education of a leader who takes the craft seriously. The results show up every time he walks back into the office. Read more about what Wisconsin's manufacturing sector needs from its leaders to understand the larger stakes of this kind of development.

Conclusion

You don't become a better leader by reading about it. You become one by doing things that require you to lead — especially when the stakes are not professional, when there's nothing in it for you, and when the people you're responsible for are counting on you to show up fully. The gym, the court, and the parish hall have taught Daniel J. Cullen as much about leadership as any conference room in Wisconsin. That's the point.

Wisconsin Manufacturing's Workforce Challenge — and How to Meet It

📅 April 2025
✎ Daniel J. Cullen
⌚ 7 min read

Wisconsin manufacturers aren't struggling because of equipment or capital. They're struggling because the people who know how to do the work are retiring, and the pipeline to replace them is nowhere near full.

Daniel J. Cullen has watched this dynamic unfold across nearly two decades in construction and metal fabrication in the Greater Waukesha area. As Director at Precision Metal Fab Inc. in Delafield, he manages it daily — the search for skilled talent, the investment required to develop it, and the longer-term thinking it takes to build a workforce that lasts beyond the current hiring cycle.

The Scale of the Problem

Wisconsin's manufacturing sector employs roughly 470,000 people — about 16% of the state's workforce — and contributes more than $60 billion annually to the state economy. It is, by any measure, the engine of Wisconsin's economic identity. And it has a serious talent problem.

The Baby Boomer retirement wave has pulled experienced machinists, welders, fabricators, and production managers out of the workforce at a pace that vocational programs and community colleges cannot match. Meanwhile, the cultural narrative around skilled trades has spent a generation steering young people toward four-year degrees, leaving the trades chronically undersold as a career path. The result is a skills gap that gets wider every year, and manufacturers in the Delafield-to-Milwaukee corridor feel it acutely.

"The shops that invest in human capital — even when it's inconvenient — are the ones still standing a decade from now."

What Employers Can Actually Control

It's tempting to frame this as a systemic problem that individual employers can't solve. That framing is both accurate and useless. The macro challenge is real, but the decisions each employer makes still determine whether they can attract, develop, and retain the people they need.

Daniel J. Cullen's view is straightforward: manufacturers who treat workforce development as someone else's job will keep losing people to the ones who don't. That means investing in structured apprenticeship and training programs, building genuine partnerships with institutions like Waukesha County Technical College — where Cullen presents regularly — and creating workplace cultures where skilled people want to stay. His approach to building a people-first manufacturing culture is a direct response to these pressures. You can read his ongoing commentary on industry trends through his writing on Substack.

The Role of Community in Workforce Development

One of the less-discussed aspects of the trades talent pipeline is how much it depends on community relationships that exist outside the factory walls. Kids who grow up around people in the trades — coaches, mentors, family members — are more likely to consider a trades career themselves. The community connections that manufacturers build today have workforce implications years down the line.

This is part of why Daniel's involvement with the Tim Cullen Institute, with youth athletics, and with Waukesha County Technical College isn't just personal — it's strategic. Employers who are visible in their communities, who mentor young people and support civic programs, are cultivating the relationships that will eventually bring talented workers through their doors. The lessons Cullen draws from his coaching work are inseparable from his thinking about industry's future.

Technology Is Not the Answer Alone

Some manufacturers respond to the labor shortage by accelerating automation — replacing workers with machines and calling it a solution. For certain processes, this makes sense. For the range of custom and precision work that defines Wisconsin's miscellaneous metals sector, it doesn't. Precision metal fabrication requires human judgment, adaptability, and craftsmanship that no current technology reliably replaces.

The answer isn't to eliminate the human element — it's to invest in the humans who provide it. Technology should augment skilled workers, not substitute for the effort of developing them. Companies that understand this distinction are building durable competitive advantages. The ones that don't are setting themselves up for quality problems, customer attrition, and operational brittleness the next time the labor market tightens.

Conclusion

Wisconsin's manufacturing workforce challenge will not be solved by any single employer, program, or policy. It requires a sustained, multi-front effort from businesses, educational institutions, community leaders, and families. But individual companies have more agency in this than they often claim. Daniel J. Cullen's work at Precision Metal Fab in Delafield is one example of what it looks like to take that agency seriously — to invest in people, build community relationships, and lead with a time horizon longer than the next quarter.
